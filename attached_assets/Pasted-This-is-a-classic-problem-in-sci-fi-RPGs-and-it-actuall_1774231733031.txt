This is a classic problem in sci-fi RPGs, and it actually plays perfectly into the gritty, "scoundrel" tone of your game. 

If characters start with enough money to buy top-tier military gear and heavy armor, they aren't hungry. And in a game called **The Ledger System**, you want your players to be hungry. 

However, players love their toys. If a Vanguard player's entire vision for their character is wearing heavy Mandalorian-style armor, locking that away for 10 sessions feels bad. 

To solve this, we give them what they want, but we make them **pay for it in narrative friction**. Here are three ways players can get extra gear at character creation, all of which generate instant plot hooks for the GM:

### 1. THE LEDGER (Taking on Debt)
If they want more than 500 credits, they can take out a loan. This directly ties into the name of your system!
* **The Mechanic:** A player may choose to take on a **[Debt]** during character creation. For every level of Debt they take, they instantly receive an extra **1,000 Credits** to spend on gear.
* **The Catch:** Debt isn't just a backstory element; it's a mechanical threat. They must choose who holds their Ledger (a Hutt cartel, the Crimson Dawn, a corrupt Imperial governor, or a Loan Shark). 
* **The Friction:** At the end of every adventure or payout, the creditor comes to collect their "interest." If the player cannot (or will not) pay a percentage of their earnings, the GM adds immediate ticks to the **Heat Track**, or the creditor sends bounty hunters to break their legs.

### 2. "HOT" OR STOLEN GEAR (The Wanted Tag)
The player didn't buy that Heavy Repeater or pristine suit of armor... they "found" it. 
* **The Mechanic:** A player can choose one piece of expensive gear or weaponry from the rulebook and get it for absolutely **Free**. 
* **The Catch:** The GM applies the **[Hot]** or **[Stolen]** tag to that specific item. It belongs to someone dangerous, and its serial numbers or transponder codes are still active.
* **The Friction:** Whenever the player uses that item in a civilized space or during a major firefight, any failure on the **Control Die** instantly generates **Heat** for the party. Eventually, the original owner is going to track them down to take it back.

### 3. JURY-RIGGED / SALVAGED GEAR (The Flawed Tag)
The player bought the gear, but they bought it out of the back of a Jawa Sandcrawler. It was cheap for a reason.
* **The Mechanic:** A player can buy any piece of equipment for **Half Price** (allowing that 500 credits to stretch much further). 
* **The Catch:** The item permanently gains the **[Jury-Rigged]** or **[Unstable]** tag. 
* **The Friction:** The gear is incredibly unreliable. If the player rolls a **1 on their Power Die** while using it, the item immediately breaks down. A weapon jams, a shield generator shorts out, or armor servos lock up. It becomes unusable until the player or an ally spends an **Action (Override/Mechanics)** to smack it back into working order mid-fight. 

---

### Why this is great for the GM:
By using these options, you never have to say "No" to a player who wants a cool toy. You just say, *"Yes, but how did you get it?"*

* If the Slinger wants the 2,000-credit custom heavy blaster, they take **Debt**, and now the GM has a built-in reason to send goons after the party.
* If the Vanguard wants the heavy armor, they take it as **Jury-Rigged**, creating amazing tactical tension in combat when their armor locks up at the worst possible moment.

It forces the players to make interesting choices before the game even starts. Do you want to implement a formal **Debt/Ledger** subsystem to track this, or just let players apply negative **Tags** to cheap gear?
